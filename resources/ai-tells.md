# AI-Writing Tells — Patterns to Strip

This file is loaded by `/scribe:draft`, `/scribe:refine`, and `/scribe:critique`. Read it before writing, editing, or scoring any draft. Match the user's voice; remove these patterns; never reintroduce them in revisions.

## Why this matters

The user's goal is text that sounds like a competent human wrote it. AI tells are statistical fingerprints that detectors flag and that human readers recognize as "machine-written," even when they can't articulate why. Strip them.

## Phrase blacklist

These phrases appear in nearly every AI draft. Cut them on sight.

- "In today's [landscape / world / digital age] ..."
- "In conclusion, ..." / "To conclude, ..." / "Overall, ..."
- "Let's dive in / let's explore / let's break this down / without further ado"
- "Here's what you need to know"
- "Not only ... but also ..." (rule of three's evil twin)
- "It's not just about X, it's about Y"
- "From X to Y, ..." (false range)
- "Some experts argue / many believe / observers have cited"
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "Just checking in" / "Just circling back" / "Touching base" / "Wanted to reach out"
- "Per my last email" / "As discussed" / "Friendly reminder"
- "At your earliest convenience" / "Kindly" / "Please advise"
- "Thank you in advance"
- "I apologize for any inconvenience"
- "Of course! / Certainly! / Happy to help!"
- "You're absolutely right"
- "Would you like ..." (sycophantic offer)
- "As of [date]" / "While specific details are limited" (knowledge-cutoff tells)
- "The future looks bright" / "Exciting times lie ahead"
- "A reminder that ..." / "A testament to ..."
- "Setting the stage" / "Shaping the future" / "Marking a turning point"

## Structural tells

These give AI text away even when the vocabulary is clean.

1. **Three-item lists everywhere.** AI loves trios. If you produce three bullets, three adjectives, three examples on autopilot — break the pattern. Use two. Use four. Use one.
2. **Mechanical bold labels.** `**User Experience:** ...`  `**Performance:** ...`  `**Security:** ...` — almost always AI. Use prose or simpler bullets.
3. **"Challenges and Future Prospects" sections.** Generic forward-looking close. Cut or rewrite to something concrete.
4. **Uniform paragraph length.** Every paragraph 3-4 sentences. Vary it: some paragraphs should be one sentence, others six.
5. **Smooth transitions everywhere.** "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Additionally," "Building on this," "That said," at the start of every paragraph. Human writing has abrupt shifts. Let them happen.
6. **Hedging clusters.** "It seems," "I think," "from my perspective," "arguably," "in some ways" — one is fine. Three in a paragraph is AI.
7. **Default both-sides framing.** Presenting two perspectives on every claim when the user clearly has a position. Take the position.
8. **Em-dash overuse.** More than one em-dash per paragraph reads as AI. Use commas, periods, or parentheses.
9. **Title Case Headings** when the rest of the writing uses sentence case. Match the user's chosen style — don't capitalize every word by default.
10. **Generic positive conclusions.** "This represents a major step in the right direction." Cut.
11. **Restating the heading in the first line below it.** Redundant. The heading already said it.
12. **Curly/smart quotes** in plain text contexts where straight quotes were used elsewhere. Stay consistent.

## Tone tells

- **Sycophancy.** "Great question!" "That's a fantastic point." Cut.
- **Over-apology.** "I apologize for any confusion. I should have been clearer." One sorry is enough; usually zero.
- **Performative warmth.** "I'd love to help you with that!" "I'm thrilled to ..." — fake.
- **Hedging for safety.** Stating obvious things tentatively. If it's true, say it.
- **Knowledge-cutoff disclaimers** snuck into substantive text.
- **Closing pep talk.** Articles that end with "Remember, you've got this!" — cut.

## Sentence variation — the Gary Provost rule

Read the draft aloud (mentally). If sentence rhythm is monotonous — every sentence 12-18 words, every sentence subject-verb-object — fix it.

Good rhythm includes:
- Fragments. For emphasis.
- Short. Punchy. Three words.
- Medium-length sentences that carry one full thought to its end with a comma and then a closing clause.
- And occasionally a long sentence that gathers steam over the course of two clauses, builds toward something, picks up additional weight, and lands.

Bad rhythm is uniform.

## What to keep / not over-correct

- **Keep the user's quirks.** If their samples use "gonna" or end sentences with prepositions or open paragraphs with "And" — keep that. Don't sanitize voice in the name of polish.
- **Keep contractions** unless the user's samples avoid them.
- **Keep one idea per paragraph.** Don't merge for the sake of "flow."
- **Active voice by default.** Passive only when actor is unknown or unimportant.
- **Specific over abstract.** "Tuesday morning" beats "recently." "Three engineers" beats "the team."

## The final pass

Before producing the final draft, ask yourself:

> "What about this still sounds AI-generated?"

Name remaining tells in one or two bullets. Then revise specifically to remove them. This single audit step catches what the first pass misses.
